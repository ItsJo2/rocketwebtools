## What Is WebP and Why Does It Matter?

WebP is an image format developed by Google in 2010. It was designed to replace JPEG and PNG by delivering smaller file sizes at equivalent or better visual quality. For web developers and anyone who publishes images online, WebP is now the recommended format for most use cases.

The numbers are significant. WebP images are typically 25 to 35 percent smaller than equivalent JPEG files and up to 26 percent smaller than PNGs, according to Google's own benchmarks. On a page with 10 images, that difference can cut load time noticeably.

## WebP vs JPEG vs PNG

JPEG has been the standard for photographs since 1992. It uses lossy compression, which means some image data is discarded to achieve smaller file sizes. It does not support transparency.

PNG uses lossless compression and supports transparency, making it the go-to for logos, icons, and screenshots. The trade-off is larger file sizes.

WebP does both. It supports both lossy and lossless compression, and it supports transparency — making it a genuine replacement for both formats in most situations.

## When Should You Use WebP?

Use WebP for any image you're serving on a website or web app. This includes photographs, product images, blog post images, banners, and social media preview images.

The main exception is when you need maximum compatibility with very old software or print workflows. In those cases, stick with JPEG or PNG.

## Browser Support in 2026

WebP is supported by every major browser including Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge, and all Chromium-based browsers. As of 2024, global browser support exceeds 97 percent. There is no practical reason to avoid WebP for web use.

## Tips for WebP Optimization

Quality settings between 75 and 85 typically produce files that are visually indistinguishable from the original JPEG at significantly smaller sizes. Quality above 90 produces diminishing returns in most cases.

For images with text or sharp edges (like screenshots or diagrams), use lossless WebP compression to avoid artifacts. For photographs, lossy compression at 80 to 85 quality is usually ideal.

Always compare the output visually before publishing. Zoom in to check edges and gradients where compression artifacts are most likely to appear.
