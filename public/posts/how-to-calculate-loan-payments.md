## How Loan Payments Are Calculated

When you take out a loan, your monthly payment is determined by three things: the principal (the amount you borrow), the interest rate, and the loan term (how long you have to repay it).

The standard formula for a fixed monthly payment is:

M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1]

Where M is the monthly payment, P is the principal, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12), and n is the total number of payments.

## A Simple Example

Suppose you borrow $10,000 at 6% annual interest for 3 years (36 months).

Monthly rate: 6% / 12 = 0.5% = 0.005
Number of payments: 36

M = 10,000 × [0.005(1.005)^36] / [(1.005)^36 - 1]
M = 10,000 × [0.005 × 1.1967] / [1.1967 - 1]
M = 10,000 × 0.005984 / 0.1967
M = 10,000 × 0.03042
M ≈ $304.22 per month

Total paid: $304.22 × 36 = $10,951.92
Total interest: $10,951.92 - $10,000 = $951.92

## What Changes When You Adjust Each Variable

**Higher principal** → higher monthly payment and more total interest paid.

**Higher interest rate** → higher monthly payment and significantly more total interest over the life of the loan.

**Longer term** → lower monthly payment but much more total interest paid. A 5-year loan at 6% costs substantially more in interest than a 3-year loan for the same amount.

## Why the Early Payments Are Mostly Interest

In the first months of a loan, most of your payment goes toward interest rather than reducing the principal. This is because interest is calculated on the remaining balance. As the balance decreases, more of each payment goes to principal.

This is called an amortization schedule. By month 30 of a 36-month loan, most of your payment is going toward principal.

## Red Flags to Watch For

Avoid loans where the interest rate is not clearly stated as an annual percentage rate (APR). Watch for origination fees, prepayment penalties, and variable rates that can change after an introductory period. Always calculate the total cost of the loan, not just the monthly payment.
