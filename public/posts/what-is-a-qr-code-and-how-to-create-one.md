## What Is a QR Code?

A QR code (Quick Response code) is a two-dimensional barcode that stores information in a grid of black and white squares. Unlike traditional barcodes which store data in one direction, QR codes store data both horizontally and vertically — allowing them to hold significantly more information in a small space.

They were invented in 1994 by Denso Wave, a Japanese automotive company, originally for tracking vehicle parts during manufacturing.

## What Can a QR Code Store?

QR codes can encode:
- URLs and website links
- Plain text
- Email addresses and pre-filled email content
- Phone numbers
- WiFi network credentials (SSID and password)
- vCard contact information
- Geographic coordinates
- Calendar events

The most common use is storing a URL that a smartphone camera can scan and open instantly.

## How QR Codes Work

The grid pattern encodes binary data using a specific structure. The three square markers in the corners help scanners orient the code regardless of angle. Error correction is built in — a QR code can still be read even if up to 30% of it is damaged or obscured, which is why you can place logos in the center of QR codes.

## Common Uses in 2026

Restaurant menus replaced physical menus with QR codes during the pandemic and most never switched back. Business cards now include QR codes linking to LinkedIn profiles or contact pages. Product packaging uses QR codes for authentication and traceability. Marketing campaigns use unique QR codes per channel to track which medium drives the most traffic.
