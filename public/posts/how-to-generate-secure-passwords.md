## What Makes a Password Weak?

Short passwords are weak. Dictionary words are weak. Passwords reused across multiple sites are weak — even if they're complex. A password that appeared in any previous data breach is compromised the moment you use it, regardless of how strong it looks.

## The Mathematics of Password Strength

Password strength is measured in bits of entropy — the number of possible combinations an attacker must try. A 6-character lowercase password has about 28 bits of entropy. A 16-character password mixing uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols has over 100 bits. The difference is not 4x harder to crack — it's billions of times harder.

## What a Strong Password Looks Like

A strong password in 2026 should be:
- At least 16 characters long
- A mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
- Completely random — not based on words, names, or dates
- Unique — never reused on another site

An example of a strong password: `X7#mK2$pL9@nQ4!w`

## Storing Your Passwords

Generating a strong password is only half the problem. You need a way to remember it. Use a password manager — applications like Bitwarden (free and open source), 1Password, or your browser's built-in manager. Never store passwords in a plain text file or spreadsheet.

## What About Passphrases?

A passphrase — four or more random words strung together like `correct-horse-battery-staple` — is easier to remember and can be just as strong as a random character password. The key word is random. Phrases that mean something to you personally are easier for attackers to guess.
